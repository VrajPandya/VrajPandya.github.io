## Opening claim

This section sets up the argument: software keeps moving closer to physical limits again. Power, memory locality, latency, and deployment footprint are no longer niche concerns. The mock should open with a direct claim, then give one concrete example from trading systems, browsers, game engines, robotics, databases, or AI infrastructure.

## Why the constraints favor C++

The first major section should make the case that the winning language is often the one that lets a team choose the exact shape of the runtime. This is where the final essay can compare garbage collection, deterministic destruction, ABI stability, and mechanical sympathy without turning into a language-war checklist.

> Placeholder pull quote: the market rewards software that can stay fast after the easy optimizations are gone.

## The installed base is not dead weight

This section should treat the existing C++ world as leverage, not legacy. Compilers, debuggers, profilers, libraries, operating-system APIs, embedded platforms, and decades of production code all make C++ hard to displace in the places where it is already trusted.

## Where the argument is weakest

The layout needs room for the counterargument. Rust, Zig, Swift, Go, Java, and managed runtimes each win real domains. The final essay should admit where C++ loses developer time, safety, onboarding speed, or build simplicity, then explain why those costs still do not remove C++ from the center of performance-critical systems.

## Closing shape

The ending should avoid triumphalism. The strongest close is probably that C++ wins by remaining useful at the boundary where abstractions meet hardware, not by becoming everyone’s favorite language.
